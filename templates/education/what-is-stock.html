What is stock?

Common stock

Within a corporation, common stock functions as a security embodying ownership. Those in possession of common stock partake in selecting the board of directors and contributing to decisions regarding corporate policies. Over an extended period, this type of equity ownership generally generates superior returns. Nevertheless, during the process of liquidation, common shareholders possess entitlement to a company's assets solely subsequent to the complete settlement of bondholders, preferred shareholders, and other debt holders. In the company's balance sheet, common stock finds representation within the stockholder's equity segment.

A company's ongoing and future profits find representation in common stock as a residual claim. This establishes shareholders as partial owners of the company. It's crucial to note that this ownership doesn't grant shareholders the ability to assert control over physical assets like chairs, desks, or computers within the company's premises. These assets are owned by the legal entity of the corporation itself. Instead, shareholders possess ownership of the residual claim itself. Investors and traders can engage in the trading of common stock on exchanges, facilitating buying and selling. Shareholders holding common stock might also be eligible to receive dividends.

The Dutch East India Company introduced the first-ever common stock in 1602, marking its debut on the Amsterdam Stock Exchange. Subsequently, stock markets have proliferated globally over the course of more than 400 years. Many thousands of companies are now listed on international stock exchanges, including prominent ones like the London Stock Exchange and the Tokyo Stock Exchange.

Major U.S.-based stocks are traded on public exchanges such as the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and NASDAQ. By Q1 2022, the NYSE had amassed 7,417 listings, boasting a combined market capitalization of approximately $53 trillion. This figure solidified its position as the world's largest stock exchange in terms of market capitalization. International stock exchanges exist for foreign stocks as well. Companies of smaller size that fail to meet listing prerequisites for exchanges are labeled as unlisted. These unlisted stocks are traded through the over-the-counter (OTC) market.

Preferred stock

Preferred stock, a specific type of stock, holds the distinction of paying shareholders a predetermined dividend. This variety of stock takes precedence over common stock when it comes to dividend distribution. Despite its nomenclature, preferred stock doesn't inherently enjoy favoritism among most investors (though its advantages are certainly notable).

In various respects, preferred stock mirrors the characteristics of a bond. To elucidate, the primary source of returns from preferred stock typically stems from its dividend. Additionally, preferred shares tend to offer a higher yield compared to common shares. Analogous to bonds, preferred stock exhibits improved performance in periods of declining interest rates. Moreover, preferred stock is assigned a par value upon issuance, which signifies its initial value and potential for redemption upon the maturity of the preferred shares.

Preferred stock further holds the potential for being "called" by the company, essentially redeemed, on a prearranged date. Consequently, the call price might surpass the original investor's purchase price. An additional distinctive aspect of select varieties of preferred stock involves their capability to transform into a fixed quantity of common shares; the opposite transformation isn't feasible. This category of stock is referred to as convertible preferred stock.

For investors with a short-term outlook, preferred stock might offer a more suitable investment choice, particularly if they are unable to retain common stock for a sufficient duration to overcome fluctuations in share prices. This is due to the relatively diminished volatility associated with preferred stock. However, it's worth noting that while preferred stock tends to experience fewer fluctuations, its potential for long-term growth is generally lower compared to common stock.
